## Triggers and Habits
**Embodied**: something that we not only know with our heads but know with our "bodies" - it comes naturally. 

There is a big difference between learning something and knowledge that is embodied. Self-help books will talk about some way that we should react to events (triggers) in the world. However, when we encounter triggers in our lives we are already inclined to react some way (habits) and invariably we will follow them This happens for two reasons:
1. We do not know (**recognition**) that we have encountered a trigger, 
2. We do not know notice our **reaction** to the trigger. 
We often hear about habits in the context of self-help. Get up at the right time, make your bed, go for a run and it'll make you more productive. However, every natural reaction we have to a trigger is habitual, including our thoughts and feelings. 
## My Experience with Therapy

"Be kind to yourself", "This situation is not as bad as I am making it out to be", "I deserve to be loved", and on the manifestation end of the spectrum - "I will be rich and handsome". Through shear force of repetition these thought patterns become habitual. However, in my opinion, the talk therapist becomes too vital in this process. Alone, I found that it was too hard to **recognise** when bad thoughts arose, let alone convince myself they were unhealthy (**evaluation**). I found the latter especially troubling. I have 18 years of Christian teaching written on my soul. Moving away from Zimbabwe was accompanied by a massive existential shift; a reshaping I am still engaging with today. I could have read more CBT books to distinguish unhealthy from healthy thought patterns, and reasoned that following their advice would "make me happy". Maybe I'm a fool but that was hardly convincing, and screamed of Western Individualism.

## The Vicious Cycle
I have made reference the problems we have but now I will formalise them in the terms that Cayoun uses, through an example of my own experiences. 
1. We encounter an event (**situation**). 
	For me, the week before exams.
2. Our body perceives the event (**sensory perception**). This not only includes our five physical senses, he defines two more. The sixth is the ability to understand the connection between a situation and our sensation - "I feel bloated because I ate a lot of food". The seventh is noticing a new thought has entered our mind. The latter is very weak in most of us, we are hardly able to notice our *changing* thoughts.  
3. The thoughts we have (**evaluation**) in reaction to the situation and the sensory perception. 
	 I will start worrying about two things. The first, is doing bad in the exam. The second, is this conception of myself that I have that I am not a hard worker. I will start to remember the other times that I wasn't able to work as hard as I thought I should have and convince myself this time won't be different.
4. (Very important) the thoughts make us feel some way. Feelings are a complex topic, and even though we talk about them a lot there is not a very good definition for what they are. According to Cayoun, whatever they are, feelings are always accompanied by a bodily sensation (**co-emergent body sensations**).  It is important that you have no choice here. There is nothing you can do *in the moment* to change this feeling. (To spoil the big reveal we can help them subside and prevent them by changing the previous step). 
	I will start to feel a burning sensation in my skin, which is the way I think my body expresses anxiety. I will start feeling an **aversion** to doing work and a **craving** to watch YouTube.
5. We take and action in response to the feelings (**reaction**). 
	I will start watching YouTube, and playing games to avoid the thoughts and feelings and experience the safety of a bodily sensation I enjoy. I won't study and I will start sleeping in, ghost my friends and missing classes (weeks at a time). 
6. We then perceive our reaction (which leads back to number 2).

According to Cayoun, there is a cycle that we encounter at this point. And if we don't stop it, it can become a spiral with every iteration making us feel worse and increase the intensity of our emotions. As a result, we do something to make the sensations better or go away. Here we give in to our cravings and aversions to make ourselves **feel** better. Cayoun proposes that this cycle is produced by too much **unhealthy evaluation** and not enough attention paid to our **sensations**. He calls this the Co-emergence Model of Reinforcement in Disequilibrium (CEMR-D).

## Mindfulness
He says that we can solve this problem in two ways:
1. Paying more and closer attention to our sensations (all **seven** senses),
2. Approaching our thoughts and sensations, our experiences, with **equanimity**.

It is these two concepts in working to together he defines as **mindfulness**. At this point it may be helpful to remove any preconceptions you have of mindfulness. Not because they may not be accurate but incase they are not. 
#### Noticing Our Sensations
This is the first part that most people are familiar with. It is concerned with the "co-emergent body sensation" and "sensory perception" sections of the CEMR. 
In this part we learn to be aware of all seven of our senses. We gain this ability by:
1. Increasing the strength and accuracy of each of them. For example, being able to notice the slightest or specific sensations - like the tension in the corner of your right eye when you think about something stressful. Or noticing that you just started thinking about the embarrassing text you sent two months ago. This is achieved through regular meditation, during which you scan every part of your body centimetre by centimetre (usually in 30 minute sessions). Initially just noticing, **equanimously**, the bodily sensations (i.e. touch) and the entrance of new thoughts into our minds. Dr. Roy, made an analogy of going to the gym to train our muscles.
2. Getting in the habit of searching our body regularly so we become more and more aware of our sensations as we go about our lives. This is done by engaging in every day activities "mindfully". Noticing our sensations as we go about doing different things, and appraising them with an equanimous mind. This is the match you have been training your muscles for. 
#### Equanimity
Here is the philosophical sticking point that most people encounter when they think about mindfulness. Admittedly, it is rooted in Buddhism - Cayoun was trained in the Burmese Vipassana tradition of Ledi Sayadaw, U Ba Khin and S. N. Goenka \[2\]. I have often come back and forth from mindfulness because I have felt it is trying convince me that the key to happiness is to stop caring. However, by considering **equanimity** as a tool I now believe that it does not teach us not to care but rather enables to care more deeply about and act in accordance with our values.

I will stop edging you reader. Cayoun derived his definition of equanimity from the Pali (language spoken in North India during the time of the Buddha) word *upekka*. 

"As the Buddha meant it, \[upekka\] is the balance and neutrality of the mind that prevents us from identifying with emerging experience." When we recognise a new sensation we will evaluate it. Cayoun claims that we must detach our identity from this new experience. 
	I realise that there is a midterm next week, and I know I must study for the midterm. I notice I am recollecting my past experiences with midterm season. At this point there are two experiences I must hold equanimously. First, the results of my midterms say nothing about who I am. Second, my past experiences with midterm season, do not make me lazy or weak. They are merely events that have and will happen. 
"In an equanimous state, the mind holds firm in remaining nonreactive despite its contact with an intense experience."
	I start to feel the burning sensation in my skin that signals my anxiety. I recognise this sensation, pay attention to it, focus on it even. Remembering, I not to attach myself to that experience.  
"Specifically, it doesn’t react with craving when the experience is pleasant or aversion when the experience is unpleasant." Our emotional pain comes from responding to our cravings and engaging in a craving-aversion cycle. When we answer a craving, that craving goes away. Inevitably, most probably withing minutes, it will be replaced by a new craving or aversion. In following this cycle we dig ourselves deeper in the CEMR-D cycle.  
	I recognise these feelings, but I do not try and run from them. I don't turn on YouTube to escape them. I notice them, without judgement, and wait for them to subside.
